  Proposed rationale (for grants/papers)

  One-sentence framing: Standard 16S-based cervicovaginal community typing cannot resolve the species-level variation within diverse (non-Lactobacillus) communities that dominate in sub-Saharan African women,
   and this resolution gap obscures the biological mechanisms linking specific bacterial species, fungi, and Mollicutes to HIV acquisition risk.

  The gap we fill: Gosmann et al. (2017) showed CT3/CT4 communities carry 4x HIV risk in FRESH — but 58% of the cohort falls into these two catch-all categories. We don't know which specific species within
  CT3/CT4 drive the risk because 16S can't resolve them. No single marker can fill this gap alone — but an integrated multi-marker approach can.

  What makes this study unique: The intersection of five elements that no other group combines:

  The logic chain that connects everything:

  Multi-marker profiling (cpn60 + pheS + ITS + Mollicute PCR)
      → Species-level cervicotype definition for SA women
          → Identify which species within CT3/CT4 drive genital inflammation
              → Link specific species/cervicotypes to HIV acquisition
                  → Longitudinal tracking reveals transition dynamics
                      → Targetable intervention points (which transitions to block)

  Significance statement: This is the first study to apply integrated multi-kingdom, multi-marker molecular profiling at species-level resolution to a longitudinal African cohort with HIV outcome data. It
  will define the species-level cervicovaginal community structures specific to young South African women, determine which specific bacterial and fungal species drive HIV acquisition risk, and establish a
  classification framework that can replace the Western-derived CST system for sub-Saharan African populations.
