# Global Cervicovaginal Community State Types: Diversity, Ethnic Variation, and Classification Gaps

**Prepared:** February 25, 2026
**Project:** Supplemental Methods for Cervicovaginal Species Cataloging

---

## 1. Introduction

The cervicovaginal microbiome plays a central role in reproductive health, influencing susceptibility to bacterial vaginosis (BV), sexually transmitted infections (STIs), human papillomavirus (HPV) persistence, preterm birth, and infertility (Ravel et al., 2011; France et al., 2022). The dominant classification framework groups cervicovaginal microbial communities into five Community State Types (CSTs), originally defined by Ravel and colleagues using 16S rRNA gene sequencing of 396 North American women of reproductive age (Ravel et al., 2011). While this framework has become the standard in the field, accumulating evidence from global populations reveals significant limitations in its universality, particularly for women of non-European descent.

This report summarizes the current understanding of CST distribution across global populations, emphasizing ethnic and geographic variation, and identifies critical gaps relevant to developing improved cataloging methods.

## 2. Community State Type Definitions

### 2.1 Overview

The five CSTs are defined by the dominant bacterial species present in the vaginal microbiome (Ravel et al., 2011; France et al., 2020):

| CST | Dominant Species | Typical pH | Stability | Health Association |
|-----|-----------------|------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **CST-I** | *Lactobacillus crispatus* | 3.5–4.2 | High | Most protective; lowest risk of BV, STIs, UTIs, preterm birth |
| **CST-II** | *Lactobacillus gasseri* | 4.0–4.4 | Moderate | Protective; relatively uncommon |
| **CST-III** | *Lactobacillus iners* | 4.2–4.6 | Low | Generally healthy but transitional; can shift to CST-IV |
| **CST-IV** | Mixed anaerobes (low *Lactobacillus*) | 4.7–5.5 | Variable | Linked to BV, cervicitis, HIV susceptibility, preterm birth |
| **CST-V** | *Lactobacillus jensenii* | 3.8–4.4 | Moderate | Protective; relatively uncommon |

### 2.2 CST-IV Subtypes

CST-IV is the most heterogeneous type and has been further subdivided (Ravel et al., 2011; Gajer et al., 2012):

- **CST-IV-A:** Colonized by anaerobes including *Sneathia amnii*, *S. sanguinegens*, *Dialister micraerophilus*, and *Atopobium vaginae*. Often associated with low Nugent scores and may be asymptomatic.
- **CST-IV-B:** Characterized by higher proportions of *Gardnerella*, *Prevotella*, *Atopobium*, *Mobiluncus*, BV-associated bacteria (BVAB1-3), and *Peptoniphilus*. Strongly associated with high Nugent scores and clinical BV.

Additional novel subtypes (IV-D0, IV-D1, IV-D2, IV-E) have been proposed based on Pacific Islander data, as discussed in Section 5.2 (Rowley et al., 2024).

 The Nugent Score

  The Nugent score is the gold standard laboratory method for diagnosing bacterial vaginosis (BV). It was developed by R.P. Nugent and colleagues in 1991 and is based on Gram stain microscopy of vaginal fluid
   — no culture, no molecular testing, just a stained smear read under a microscope.

  How it's measured

  A vaginal swab is rolled onto a glass slide, heat-fixed, Gram-stained, and examined under oil immersion (1000x). The microscopist scores three bacterial morphotypes:

  ┌───────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────────┐
  │        Morphotype         │        What you're looking at        │              Represents               │
  ├───────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Large Gram-positive rods  │ Thick, purple rods                   │ Lactobacillus spp.                    │
  ├───────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Small Gram-variable rods  │ Thin, variable-staining coccobacilli │ Gardnerella / Bacteroides (anaerobes) │
  ├───────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Curved Gram-variable rods │ Comma-shaped bacteria                │ Mobiluncus spp.                       │
  └───────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────────┘

  Each morphotype is scored 0–4 based on how many you see per oil immersion field:

  ┌───────┬────────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────┐
  │ Score │ Lactobacillus (large rods) │ Gardnerella/Bacteroides (small rods) │ Mobiluncus (curved rods) │
  ├───────┼────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ 0     │ 30+ per field              │ 0                                    │ 0                        │
  ├───────┼────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ 1     │ 5–30                       │ <1                                   │ <1                       │
  ├───────┼────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ 2     │ 1–4                        │ 1–4                                  │ 1–4                      │
  ├───────┼────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ 3     │ <1                         │ 5–30                                 │ 5–30                     │
  ├───────┼────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
  │ 4     │ 0                          │ 30+                                  │ 30+                      │
  └───────┴────────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────┘

  Note the inverse scoring: Lactobacillus scoring is reversed — more Lactobacilli = lower score. More anaerobes = higher score.

  Total Nugent Score = Lactobacillus score + Gardnerella/Bacteroides score + Mobiluncus score

  Range: 0–10

### 2.3 Functional Significance

*Lactobacillus*-dominated communities (CSTs I, II, III, V) maintain low vaginal pH through lactic acid production, which inhibits pathogen colonization. However, not all *Lactobacillus* species are equally protective:

- *L. crispatus* (CST-I) produces both D- and L-lactic acid and is the strongest protector against BV and STIs (France et al., 2022).
- *L. iners* (CST-III) produces only L-lactic acid, has a smaller genome, and is considered a transitional species that can coexist with BV-associated bacteria (France et al., 2020; Petrova et al., 2017).

## 3. Ethnic and Racial Variation in CST Distribution — North American Data

### 3.1 CST Prevalence by Self-Identified Race/Ethnicity

The foundational data on ethnic variation comes from Ravel et al. (2011), with subsequent confirmation by multiple US-based studies:

| CST | White | Asian (US) | Hispanic | Black / African American |
|-----|-------|------------|----------|--------------------------|
| **CST-I** (*L. crispatus*) | ~40% | ~35–40% | ~20–25% | ~15–18% |
| **CST-II** (*L. gasseri*) | ~10–15% | ~15–20% | ~5–10% | ~5–8% |
| **CST-III** (*L. iners*) | ~25–30% | ~25–30% | ~25–30% | ~35–40% |
| **CST-IV** (diverse anaerobes) | ~9% | ~18% | ~34% | ~39% |
| **CST-V** (*L. jensenii*) | ~5–10% | ~5–10% | ~5% | ~3–5% |

### 3.2 Lactobacillus Dominance Summary

| Population | % with Lactobacillus-dominated CST (I+II+III+V) |
|-----------|------------------------------------------------|
| White | ~90% |
| Asian (US) | ~80% |
| Hispanic | ~60% |
| Black / African American | ~60% |

### 3.3 Vaginal pH by Ethnicity

Mean vaginal pH differs significantly across ethnic groups (Ravel et al., 2011):

| Population | Mean Vaginal pH |
|-----------|----------------|
| White | 4.2 +/- 0.3 |
| Asian (US) | 4.4 +/- 0.59 |
| Black | 4.7 +/- 1.04 |
| Hispanic | 5.0 +/- 0.59 |

### 3.4 Confounding by Social Determinants

Recent work has challenged the assumption that these differences are primarily biological. In a large pregnancy cohort (ECHO Consortium), lower educational attainment — rather than self-identified race — remained a robust predictor of CST-III and CST-IV after adjustment for multiple demographic variables (Gerson et al., 2024). Factors such as stress, hygiene product access, healthcare access, and socioeconomic status may contribute more substantially than genetic ancestry to CST distribution.

## 4. South American Data

### 4.1 Brazil

The largest South American CST study examined 609 reproductive-age women across five regions of Brazil using 16S rRNA V3-V4 sequencing (Marconi et al., 2020):

| CST | Dominant Species | Prevalence |
|-----|-----------------|------------|
| **CST-I** | *L. crispatus* | 30.5% |
| **CST-II** | *L. gasseri* | 4.4% |
| **CST-III** | *L. iners* | 36.5% |
| **CST-IV** | Mixed anaerobes | 27.4% |
| **CST-V** | *L. jensenii* | 1.2% |

Approximately 64% of Brazilian women harbored either *L. iners*-dominated (CST-III) or Lactobacillus-depleted (CST-IV) communities — the two types considered least protective. This distribution mirrors that observed in African American and Hispanic US populations more than in White or Asian groups, consistent with Brazil's highly admixed population.

Independent risk factors for CST-IV included smoking (OR 1.80; 95% CI 1.02–3.18), number of sexual partners (OR 2.11; 95% CI 1.20–3.70), and vaginal douching (OR 2.24; 95% CI 1.34–3.74). Protective factors included dairy intake and sitz bathing (Marconi et al., 2020).

### 4.2 Other South American Countries

Dedicated CST-classification studies from Colombia, Peru, Chile, Argentina, and other South American nations are largely absent from the published literature. Given the genetic, cultural, and environmental diversity across the continent, this represents a significant gap.

## 5. Oceanian Data

### 5.1 Aboriginal Australian Women

A study comparing the genital microbiomes of pregnant Aboriginal and non-Aboriginal women in Australia revealed distinct community profiles (Brabin et al., 2020):

| Feature | Aboriginal Women | Non-Aboriginal Women |
|---------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Dominant Lactobacillus** | *L. iners* (CST-III pattern) | *L. crispatus* (CST-I pattern) |
| **Enriched taxa** | *Atopobium*, *Corynebacterium*, *Megasphaera*, *Ureaplasma* | *Anaerococcus*, *Enterococcus*, *Prevotella*, *Streptococcus* |
| **Atopobium in low-Lactobacillus communities** | Common, NOT associated with clinical disease | Less common |

A critical finding was that *Atopobium* — typically considered a BV-associated organism — was prevalent in low-Lactobacillus communities of Aboriginal women without corresponding clinical signs of disease. This challenges the assumption that non-*Lactobacillus*-dominated communities are inherently pathological.

### 5.2 Pacific Islander Women — Fiji

Rowley et al. (2024) performed metagenomic shotgun sequencing on 258 vaginal and 92 paired endocervical samples from women in Fiji, producing one of the most significant findings for CST classification:

- **36% of vaginal samples** and **41% of endocervical samples** could not be classified using the standard VALENCIA subCST reference framework.
- Four novel subCSTs were proposed to accommodate these communities:
  - **IV-D0, IV-D1, IV-D2:** Differentially dominated by *Gardnerella vaginalis*, *Prevotella* spp., and *L. iners*.
  - **IV-E:** A distinct mixed community with no equivalent in existing classification systems.
- The study was conducted in a population with 30–44% *Chlamydia trachomatis* prevalence among young women. CT-infected endocervical microbiomes showed smaller microbial networks lacking interactions with beneficial *Lactobacillus* species.

This finding demonstrates that the existing CST framework is insufficient for classifying cervicovaginal communities in Pacific Islander populations and likely other underrepresented groups.

## 6. Sub-Saharan African Data

### 6.1 East and Southern Africa

Longitudinal studies from Kenya, South Africa, and Rwanda report that among women with Lactobacillus-positive communities, *L. iners* dominates approximately 75% of cases, with *L. crispatus* present in ~35% but rarely as the sole dominant species (Anahtar et al., 2015; van de Wijgert et al., 2014). CST-IV prevalence is elevated compared to European populations, but notably, many women with CST-IV communities are asymptomatic.

### 6.2 West Africa

Studies from Burkina Faso identified three vaginal microbial clusters: (1) *L. iners*-dominated (77%), with minor contributions from *L. crispatus* (11%), *L. fornicalis* (3.9%), and *L. gasseri* (3.2%); (2) coagulase-negative *Staphylococcus*-dominated communities; and (3) CST-IV-type communities dominated by *Gardnerella* species (Mehta et al., 2020).

A Nigerian study found no remarkable differences between the vaginal microbiomes of Nigerian and Swedish women, suggesting that geographic and lifestyle factors may matter more than continental ancestry alone (Ogun et al., 2020).

### 6.3 Research Gaps in Africa

A systematic review of vaginal microbiome studies in sub-Saharan Africa (Teka et al., 2020) revealed important methodological gaps: many studies used culture-based or low-resolution molecular methods, and next-generation sequencing studies remain concentrated in a small number of countries (South Africa, Kenya, Rwanda). The Vaginal Microbial Genome Collection showed significantly lower metagenomic mapping rates for samples from Africa compared to Europe and North America, indicating systematic underrepresentation of African-associated taxa in reference databases (Chen et al., 2023).

---

## 7. East Asian Data

Women of East Asian descent generally show CST distributions similar to those of European-ancestry women, with high rates of *Lactobacillus* dominance and relatively low CST-IV prevalence (~18% in US-based studies). CST-I (*L. crispatus*) is common in Chinese and Japanese populations (Ravel et al., 2011). However, metagenomic studies from China report lower reference genome mapping rates compared to European cohorts, suggesting uncharacterized microbial diversity even within Lactobacillus-dominant communities (Chen et al., 2023).

## 9. Implications for Improved Cataloging Methods

### 9.1 Limitations of the Current CST Framework

1. **Population bias:** The original CST framework was developed using predominantly North American women of European descent. Over one-third of Pacific Islander samples cannot be classified within it (Rowley et al., 2024).
2. **Pathology assumptions:** CST-IV is considered dysbiotic by the framework, yet asymptomatic CST-IV communities are common in Aboriginal Australian and sub-Saharan African women (Brabin et al., 2020; Anahtar et al., 2015).
3. **Reference database gaps:** Metagenomic mapping rates are significantly lower for samples from non-European populations, indicating uncharacterized species and strains (Chen et al., 2023).
4. **Transitional states:** *L. iners* (CST-III) is the most globally common Lactobacillus species but provides less protection than *L. crispatus*, and its role varies by population context.

### 9.2 Requirements for Supplemental Methods

Any improved cataloging approach should:

- Accommodate community types not captured by CSTs I–V, including the novel IV-D and IV-E subtypes identified in Fiji.
- Distinguish between pathological and non-pathological low-Lactobacillus communities based on functional markers rather than taxonomy alone.
- Incorporate reference genomes from underrepresented populations, particularly sub-Saharan Africa, Oceania, and South America.
- Consider strain-level variation within species (e.g., different *L. iners* or *Gardnerella* strains may have different health implications).
- Account for social determinants that confound racial/ethnic categories.
